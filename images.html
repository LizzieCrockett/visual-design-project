Image 1

• This is a portrait of Aphra Behn by Sir Peter Lely, which I edited in Photoshop.
• I wanted it for the background image of my site, so I resized it to 3262x2732 so that the face would be in the correct proportion on the page.
• It is a photograph of a painting, and has a somewhat faded appearance - I have never seen the original painting so don’t know if this fading is on the painting or the photo.
• In order to subtly remove this faded and washed-out effect, I brought the brightness down to -5 from original, and the contrast up +25.
• To further freshen the image, I adjusted the colour balance in the highlights 15 points from cyan to red, creating a rosier tint.
• The number of colours and extensive colour gradations in this image meant it made sense to save it as a jpg.
• When saving for web, I opted for medium quality. The quality of the image needed to be balanced against its very large size, but the grainy nature of the oil painting meant crispness wasn’t a big problem, and the image only began to lose detail when quality was set to low.

Image 2

• This is a photograph of Aphra Behn’s grave in Westminster Abbey, which I edited in Photoshop.
• Its intended use is as a 16:9 image (576x324) in a modular content box on the website.
• Under different circumstances, I would have gone to Westminster Abbey and taken a photo in better lighting. However, due to the COVID-19 lockdown, this was not possible, so I used this as a challenge to optimise the image I have to the best of my ability.
• The image contains a lot of detail I didn’t need, and I wanted to focus on the meaningful content, which in this context was the text on the grave.
• I first used the Crop tool to select a 16:9 ratio (which framed the text details beautifully).
• I then straightened it in the Crop tool, and resized it to 576x324.
• Reducing the brightness by 10 and increasing the contrast by 10 brought out the letters into sharper detail.
• Colour adjustment in the midtones of 25 points from yellow to blue introduced a slightly cooler wash.
• To reduce the glare on the bottom right-hand corner, I masked the image and did a targeted curves adjustment in that corner.
• I then used the Clone Stamp tool to remove the scuff between the words "Aphra" and "April".
• Finally, I applied a black and white filter, as I felt this sharpened the effect and made the image more striking.
• It was saved for web as jpg, as with the original image. The small size of the image could be balanced against the quality. In this case it was important to retain as much quality as possible due to the image being not ideal in the first place. At medium quality, the finer details of the lettering (especially the 9 in "1689") became a little less clear, but no noticeable improvement was gained at maximum quality, so I opted to save it at high quality.

Image 3

• This is an image of some English civil war-era propaganda, which I sourced as a 500x306 png.
• Its intended use was in a 16:9, 576x324 content box, as with Image 2.
• When enlarging the original image, there is some blurring and pixelation on the lines, and there is also what looks like a faint watermark across the centre of the image.
• I used Illustrator to edit this image as I wanted to use the Image Trace tool to sharpen the lines and create more of a "woodcut" effect.
• I used Black and White Image Trace mode with paths and corners set to maximum and noise set to minimum to create this effect.
• Since Image Trace can also render a transparent background, I was also able to remove the watermark and insert a plain white background.
• I would say the resulting image has a different effect to the original, as opposed to being an improvement upon it.
• I saved it for web in PNG-8, as it is a graphic drawing. It is black and white, so few colours are required, and it only began to lose quality when selecting fewer than 8 colours.
• I then cropped the image in Photoshop and resized to 576x324 for use on the site.

Image 4

• This is a photo of an edition of Aphra Behn’s published works. I liked the wording and wanted to use it, but in a stylised form.
• I edited the image in Photoshop and then Illustrator to achieve the effects I wanted.
• Initially I cropped the image to only contain the title text, and straightened it.
• I then raised the contrast to 100 to sharpen the image.
• I reduced the yellowed effect on the paper by making a colour adjustment, moving yellow 20 points to blue.
• I then used the Clone Stamp tool to remove the stained marks on the paper. The image at this intermediate stage can be seen below.
• I then used the Image Trace tool in Illustrator, using 3 Colours mode to create the cartoonish, stylised effect.
• It was saved for web as a PNG-8 wth 4 colour setting - since the Image Trace had reduced it to three colours, there was no loss of quality in using minimal colours.

Image 5

• This is a logo for the whole project, designed to be used as a splash loading page on the website, and in other locations as needed.
• I created it in Illustrator.
• The typefaces used are IM Fell Double Pica (for lowercase) and IM Fell Double Pica SC (for uppercase), in keeping with the choice made for the website mock-ups.
• To create the very specific layout of the letters, I made them separate elements and repositioned them.
• I wanted the letter A to have a vertical rather than diagonal line on the right side, and I achieved this by using the Shear tool to manipulate its shape.
• To ensure the text displayed correctly on devices without the font IM Fell installed, when exporting I used the option Convert to Outline, to ensure it was processed as an image rather than text.
• I also made a favicon for the website with a monogram "AB", using the same sheared A.

Image 6

• This image was inspired by a quote from Germaine Greer. In saying we know almost nothing about Behn's private life, she described her as "a palimpsest; she has scratched herself out." This conveyed a very powerful visual image to me, so I created this image in Photoshop to reflect it.
• The source image is an engraving of a portrait by Mary Beale, and is very small at 400x320.
• To make the light and dark parts "pop" more and make the image less faded, I reduced the brightness by -20 and increased the contrast by +50.
• I then altered the colour of the grey pixels to create a richer, inky blue effect.
• I created the scratch effect using the Paint tool with a 1px brush on a hard round setting. I experimented with black and various shades of grey, but white was the most striking effect.
• The phrase "scratched herself out" suggests a frenetic or impulsive mood, so I wanted the text to appear slightly irregular on the image - the words are not laid out to align perfectly.
• I experimented with breaking up the word "scratched" into "scr atch ed" and scattering it across the page, but this made the effect too random, so I kept it as one word.
• I chose a typewriter-style typeface, Special Elite, for the main text, as it conveys a continuity with a modern idea of being a writer, thus placing Behn in a more modern context.
• It took me a long time to find the right typeface for "scratched." I experimented with a number of rough cursive-style fonts, but no matter how sharp-edged, they always seemed too "friendly" for the effect I wanted to convey. Unexpectedly, a plainer, more open sans-serif typeface worked best. I ultimately settled on Gruppo.
• The image is an engraving and does not have a great deal of colour gradation, so I saved it for web as a PNG-8. I opted for 64 colours, as below this, the image began to lose colours.
